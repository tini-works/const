# Story 01 — Returning Patient Recognition

## Customer's Words

> "Every time I visit the clinic, the receptionist asks me the same questions. My allergies, my insurance, my address. I already told you last time. It's annoying and it feels like nobody remembers me."

## What the Customer Is Saying

Three things, in order of emotional weight:

1. **"Nobody remembers me"** — The patient feels anonymous. They have a relationship with this clinic (they return), but the clinic doesn't act like it. This is the core wound. It's not a data problem to them — it's a relationship problem.

2. **"I already told you last time"** — There's an expectation that information given once persists. The patient assumes the system works. They're confused that it doesn't.

3. **"Same questions — allergies, insurance, address"** — These are the specific data points they're asked to repeat. The patient named three categories. There may be more they didn't mention, but these are the ones that crossed the annoyance threshold.

## What the Customer Is NOT Saying

- They are not asking to skip the visit flow entirely. They still expect to check in.
- They are not saying "don't ask me anything." They'd likely accept a quick confirmation ("is your address still X?").
- They are not describing a first visit. This is a returning patient. The first-visit flow is a different story.
- They didn't mention medical history, medications, or visit-specific intake questions. Those may be acceptable to re-ask. The complaint is about static personal data being re-collected.

## Natural Zoom Level

The customer sees one event: **"I visit the clinic."** Inside that event is a sub-event: **"I check in at reception."** Inside that is the pain: **"I'm asked things you already know."**

The customer's zoom level is the check-in moment at reception for a returning visit.
